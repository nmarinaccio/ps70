Week 3: Kinetic Sculpture

This week’s assignment was to design and build a small kinetic sculpture using basic hand tools and laser-cut parts, then power it with simple circuitry (e.g., a DC motor and a supply). The goal is to explore mechanisms, motion, and assembly while practicing documentation of process and results.

I started by collecting ideas that could translate into repeatable motion with simple parts and constraints. Below are three early directions I explored.

The Ideation Stage

The Chairlift

When I first thought about a kinetic sculpture, I wanted to make something similar to a mechanism I need for my final project: a motor pulling tension on a door handle via line. That led to a spinning loop concept—combining sculpture with a ski-chairlift motif. But a few issues surfaced: preventing belt/line slip on the loop, maintaining an uphill incline, and fabricating a robust motor connector without 3D printing. Those risks made this path less attractive.

The Moving Sidewalk

Next, I considered a wider belt with a textured surface to shuttle objects and “kick” them off an end stop. This would address slipping and incline issues, but the motor coupling—again without 3D-printed hubs—remained finicky. Doable, but not how I wanted to spend the week.

The Dancing Tree

After a very busy week, I pivoted to a decorative “dancing tree.” The idea was to get a simple, reliable motion working first—then layer on flair (figurines, ornaments) later. Function first, polish second.

The Development

Starting with the CAD, I made concentric circles 40 mm thick from the outside inward, with a 5 mm center bore for the dowel. I then designed gear-shaped central holders (kept for aesthetics) and added arms for each level, rotated 90° between tiers.

After cutting, I mounted the system on a 5.5 mm dowel. A snug fit was achieved by reducing the hole slightly with cardboard. I cut a slit at the dowel base to mate with the motor’s output shape, then assembled the motor, arms, and dowel with careful spacing and hot glue. First test: success.

The structure needed to support both the motor and rotating tree while clearing a dual-ended shaft. I built a two-chamber box: the top cradles the motor and provides through-slots for the axle; the bottom can house a future battery pack. Final touches included a zip tie for restraint and a centering slit to keep the tree aligned.

Final Product

Here’s the result. I only had time to paint stripes on one tier, and there’s room for more décor, but the motion works reliably! Next steps: switch to wood for stiffness, slightly widen the support arms, and add decorative elements. For a one-week build, I’m happy with how it turned out.
